Problem 2

The value of $\sqrt{7+\sqrt{7-\sqrt{7+\sqrt{7-...\infty}}}}$

Solution

Let $$\sqrt{7+\sqrt{7-\sqrt{7+\sqrt{7-...\infty}}}}=x$$ Square on Both Sides.$$7+\sqrt{7-\sqrt{7+\sqrt{7-\sqrt{7+...\infty}}}}=x^2$$ $$x^2-7=\sqrt{7-\sqrt{7+\sqrt{7-\sqrt{7...\infty}}}}$$ $$(x^2-7)^2=7-\sqrt{7+\sqrt{7-\sqrt{7...\infty}}}$$ $$(x^2-7)^2-7=-x$$ (Expanding) $$x^4-14x^2+x+42=0$$ Trying out $x=2$, $$12-56+2+42 = 4 \neq 0$$ Trying out $x=-2$, $$12-56-2+42 = 0$$ $\therefore x=-2$ is one of the roots.
Divide $x^4-14x^2+x+42=0$ using $x+2$ using synthetic division. \begin{array}{c|rrrrr} -2 & 1 & 0 & -14 & 1 & 42\\ & 0 & -2 & 4 & 20 & -42\\ \hline & 1 & -2 & -10 & 21 & 0\\ \end{array} $$\therefore x^4-14x^2+x+42=0 = (x+2)(x^3-2x^2-10x+21)$$ To find roots of $(x^3-2x^2-10x+21)$, Trying out $x=3$, $$27-18-30+21=48-48=0$$ $\therefore x=3$ is one of the roots. Divide $x^3-2x^2-10x+21=0$ using $x-3$ using synthetic division. \begin{array}{c|rrrr} 3 & 1 & -2 & -10 & 21\\ & 0 & 3 & 3 & -21\\ \hline & 1 & 1 & -7 & 0\\ \end{array} $$\therefore x^4-14x^2+x+42=0 = (x+2)(x-3)(x^2+x-7)$$ Using Quadratic formula finding the roots of $x^2+x-7$, $$x=\frac{-1\pm\sqrt{29}}{3}$$ Therefore, all the possible values of x are, $$x=-2,3,\frac{-1+\sqrt{29}}{3},\frac{-1-\sqrt{29}}{3}$$ Since $\sqrt{7+\sqrt{7-\sqrt{7+\sqrt{7-...\infty}}}}$ is under a square root, it cannot be negative, $x$ has to be positive. $\therefore$The only possible Solution is, $$x=3$$
