Problem 8

If $\theta = \frac{\pi}{2^x+1}$, show that, $\cos\theta\cos2\theta\cos4\theta....\cos2^{x-1}\theta=\frac{1}{2^x}$

Solution

Rewrite the equation into, $$2^x(\cos\theta\cos2\theta\cos4\theta....\cos2^{x-1}\theta)=1$$ Now taking the left hand side of the equation, $$2^x(\cos\theta\cos2\theta\cos4\theta....\cos2^{x-1}\theta)$$ $$=(2\cos\theta) (2\cos2\theta)(2\cos4\theta)....(2\cos2^{x-1} \theta)$$ Multiplying and dividing by $\sin\theta$, $$=\frac{1}{\sin\theta}(2\sin\theta\cos\theta)(2\cos2\theta)(2\cos4\theta)....(2\cos2^{x-1} \theta)$$ $$=\frac{1}{\sin\theta}(2\sin2\theta\cos2\theta)(2\cos4\theta)(2\cos8\theta)....(2\cos2^{x-1} \theta)$$ $$=\frac{1}{\sin\theta}(2\sin4\theta\cos4\theta)(2\cos8\theta)(2\cos16\theta)....(2\cos2^{x-1} \theta)$$ $$.$$ $$.$$ $$.$$ $$.$$ $$=\frac{2^{x-(x-1)}}{\sin\theta}\sin2^{x-1}\theta\cos2^{x-1}\theta=\frac{2\sin2^{x-1}\theta\cos2^{x-1}\theta}{\sin\theta}$$ $$=\frac{\sin{2^x}\theta}{\sin\theta}\label{one} \tag{1}$$ Note: $\sin\theta=\sin(\pi-\theta)$ $$\sin2^x\theta=\sin(\pi-2^x\theta)$$ Given, $\theta=\frac{\pi}{2^x+1}$ $$\sin\left(\pi-2^x\left(\frac{\pi}{2^x+1}\right)\right)=\sin\left(\frac{\cancel{2^x\pi}+\pi-\cancel{2^x\pi}}{2^x+1}\right)$$ $$=\sin\left(\frac{\pi}{2^x+1}\right)=\sin\theta$$ $$\therefore\sin2^x\theta=\sin\theta \label{two} \tag{2}$$ Subsituting $\eqref{two}$ in $\eqref{one}$, $$\frac{\sin\theta}{\sin\theta}=1$$

Hence Proved.
